Chapter 2: Is Matter Around Us Pure

üîπ Pure Substance

A pure substance is a material which contains only one type of particles. Example: Gold, Oxygen, Distilled water

üîπ Mixture

A mixture is formed when two or more substances are mixed together in any ratio. Example: Air, Salt solution, Soil

üîπ Types of Mixtures

  • Homogeneous mixture: Composition is uniform throughout (e.g. salt in water)
  • Heterogeneous mixture: Composition is not uniform (e.g. sand in water)

üîπ Solution

A solution is a homogeneous mixture of two or more substances.

  • Solute ‚Äì substance that dissolves
  • Solvent ‚Äì substance that dissolves the solute

üîπ Suspension

A heterogeneous mixture in which particles are visible and settle down. Example: Muddy water

üîπ Colloid

A heterogeneous mixture whose particles do not settle down. Example: Milk, Fog

üîπ Separation of Mixtures

  • Handpicking
  • Filtration
  • Evaporation
  • Distillation
  • Chromatography

üìò NCERT Tip: Pure substances have fixed composition, mixtures do not.

Chapter 3: Atoms and Molecules

üîπ Introduction

All matter is made up of very tiny particles called atoms and molecules. This chapter explains laws of chemical combination, atoms, molecules, and how we calculate mass in chemical reactions.

üîπ Laws of Chemical Combination

1. Law of Conservation of Mass

Mass can neither be created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction.

Example: Total mass of reactants = Total mass of products

2. Law of Constant Proportions

A chemical compound always contains the same elements in the same fixed proportion by mass.

Example: Water always has hydrogen and oxygen in the ratio 1:8 by mass.

üîπ Dalton‚Äôs Atomic Theory

  • All matter is made up of atoms.
  • Atoms are indivisible particles.
  • Atoms of the same element are identical.
  • Atoms combine in simple whole-number ratios.

üîπ What is an Atom?

An atom is the smallest particle of an element that takes part in a chemical reaction.

Chapter 4: Structure of the Atom

Introduction

Atoms are the basic building blocks of matter. Everything around us is made up of atoms. Scientists proposed different models of the atom to understand its structure.

Dalton‚Äôs Atomic Theory

  • All matter is made of very small particles called atoms.
  • Atoms are indivisible and cannot be created or destroyed.
  • Atoms of the same element are identical.
  • Atoms combine in fixed ratios to form compounds.

Discovery of Electron

J.J. Thomson discovered electrons using cathode ray experiments. Electrons are negatively charged particles present inside atoms.

Thomson‚Äôs Model of Atom

According to this model, an atom is a positively charged sphere with electrons embedded in it like raisins in a pudding.

Discovery of Nucleus

Rutherford conducted the gold foil experiment and discovered that:

  • Most of the atom is empty space.
  • All positive charge is concentrated at the center called nucleus.
  • Electrons revolve around the nucleus.

Bohr‚Äôs Model of Atom

Niels Bohr proposed that electrons revolve around the nucleus in fixed energy levels called shells.

Neutrons

Neutrons are neutral particles present in the nucleus along with protons.

Atomic Number

Atomic number is the number of protons present in the nucleus of an atom.

Mass Number

Mass number is the total number of protons and neutrons in an atom.

Isotopes

Isotopes are atoms of the same element having same atomic number but different mass numbers.

Isobars

Isobars are atoms of different elements having same mass number but different atomic numbers.

Uses of Isotopes

  • Cobalt-60 is used in cancer treatment.
  • Carbon-14 is used in carbon dating.

Chapter 5: The Fundamental Unit of Life

1. Introduction

All living organisms are made up of cells. The cell is the basic structural, functional, and biological unit of life.

2. Discovery of Cells

  • Robert Hooke (1665) observed cells in cork tissue and coined the term "cell".
  • Anton van Leeuwenhoek observed living cells (microorganisms) under his microscope.

3. Cell Theory

The cell theory was proposed by Matthias Schleiden and Theodor Schwann. Main points:

  • All organisms are made of one or more cells.
  • The cell is the basic unit of life.
  • All cells arise from pre-existing cells (Rudolf Virchow).

4. Types of Cells

  • Prokaryotic Cells: No nucleus (e.g., bacteria), simple structure.
  • Eukaryotic Cells: Nucleus present, complex (e.g., plant and animal cells).

5. Structure of a Cell

Plant Cell

  • Cell Wall: Provides support and protection.
  • Cell Membrane: Controls movement of substances in/out of cell.
  • Nucleus: Controls cell activities, contains DNA.
  • Cytoplasm: Jelly-like substance where organelles are suspended.
  • Chloroplasts: Site of photosynthesis, contains chlorophyll.
  • Mitochondria: Powerhouse of the cell, produces energy.
  • Vacuole: Stores water, nutrients, and waste.

Animal Cell

  • No cell wall.
  • Small vacuoles.
  • Contains all organelles like nucleus, mitochondria, etc.

6. Cell Organelles

  • Nucleus ‚Äì Control center
  • Mitochondria ‚Äì Energy production
  • Endoplasmic Reticulum ‚Äì Transport system
  • Ribosomes ‚Äì Protein synthesis
  • Golgi Apparatus ‚Äì Packaging & secretion
  • Lysosomes ‚Äì Digestive enzymes
  • Chloroplasts ‚Äì Photosynthesis (in plants)
  • Vacuole ‚Äì Storage

7. Cell Division

  • Cells divide for growth and repair.
  • Types: Mitosis (for growth/repair) and Meiosis (for reproduction).

8. Summary

The cell is the structural and functional unit of life. Both plant and animal cells have various organelles that perform different functions necessary for life.
